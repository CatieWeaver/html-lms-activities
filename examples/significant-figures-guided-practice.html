1 Foundation

Where do significant figures come from?

Before looking at rules, it helps to understand the core idea — and why every measurement carries a built-in limit on how precisely it can be reported.

Core Idea

Every measurement is only as precise as the instrument that recorded it. Significant figures are a standardized way of honestly communicating that precision — reporting exactly what your instrument told you, no more and no less. When those measurements go into calculations, sig figs ensure the result doesn't claim more certainty than the original data can support.

The same object, measured by two different instruments

The precision of your reported value depends entirely on which tool you used.

Ruler A — centimeter markings only
2.8 cm
Last digit (8) is estimated between cm marks.
This measurement has 2 sig figs.
Ruler B — millimeter markings
2.76 cm
Last digit (6) is estimated between mm marks.
This measurement has 3 sig figs.

The takeaway: Both readings are correct — they simply reflect the precision of the instrument used. When measured values go into calculations, significant figures carry that precision forward honestly.

Significant figures work the same way in any context — what matters is how precisely the original value was recorded. Let's look at another example: Streaming royalties are how musicians earn money each time someone plays their song on platforms like Spotify. The two artists below both earn royalties — but at very different scales.

Artist A — a major-label artist

1 Reported annual royalties: $1,200,000 — rounded to the nearest hundred thousand.
2 The label deducts $43,750 in fees:
$1,200,000
− $43,750
──────────────
$1,156,250 ← calculator
3 Round to the nearest hundred thousand. The ten-thousands digit is 5, so round up.
Result: $1,200,000 — no change.
The fees are real, but the reported earnings were never precise enough to detect them.

Artist B — an independent artist

1 Reported annual royalties: $86,000 — rounded to the nearest thousand.
2 Pays $12,500 in platform and distribution fees:
$86,000
− $12,500
───────────
$73,500 ← calculator
3 Round to the nearest thousand. The hundreds digit is 5, so round up.
Result: $74,000 — the fees register.
The same concept that made Artist A's fees disappear turns Artist B's fees into a $12,000 reported loss. Precision level determined what showed up — not the size of the fee.

The pattern — and what it means in the lab: In the example above, the earnings were recorded at different levels of precision, and that single variable determined everything. A larger fee vanished because the reported salary was too broad to detect it. A smaller fee registered because the reported salary was precise enough to show it. In your lab work, this plays out every time you combine measurements from instruments with different precision levels. The less precise instrument always sets the ceiling.

2 Reference

The four rules for identifying sig figs

Keep these handy as you work through the practice problems. In each example, significant digits are highlighted and non-significant digits appear in gray.

1

All nonzero digits

Every digit from 1 through 9 is always significant — no exceptions.

4726 → 4 sig figs

When zeros appear, Rules 2–4 determine whether they count.

2

Captive zeros

Zeros sandwiched between nonzero digits are always significant.

6049 → 4 sig figs

It cannot be removed without changing the value — it is captured between two real digits.

3

Leading zeros

Zeros at the front of a decimal number are not significant — they only tell you how small the number is, not how precisely it was measured.

0.00512 → 3 sig figs

These zeros appear before any measured digit — they are positioning guides, not data.

4

Trailing zeros

Zeros at the end of a number are significant only if a decimal point is present — the decimal point signals that those zeros were actually measured.

8600  → 2 sig figs
8600. → 4 sig figs
8600.00 → 6 sig figs

The decimal point is a deliberate signal: "I measured those zeros." Without it, trailing zeros are assumed to be placeholders only.

4 Practice — Part B

Applying sig figs in calculations

Calculations follow two different rules depending on the operation. Work through each problem in two steps: first identify which rule applies, then select the correctly rounded answer.

Quick reference —
Addition & Subtraction: Round your answer to the same number of decimal places as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.
Multiplication & Division: Round your answer to the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest sig figs.

5 Summary

Key takeaways

Identifying sig figs

  • All nonzero digits always count
  • Captive zeros always count
  • Leading zeros never count
  • Trailing zeros: only with a decimal point
  • More zeros after the decimal = more sig figs

Addition & subtraction

  • Focus on decimal places, not sig fig count
  • Match the least precise measurement
  • Stack numbers to find the limiting column
  • Round the final answer — don't just cut off the extra digits

Multiplication & division

  • Focus on sig fig count, not decimal places
  • Match the measurement with fewest sig figs
  • Your calculator returns every digit — it's your job to apply the sig fig rules and round the result

You started this activity with two rulers measuring the same sample. Every practice problem you just worked through is the downstream consequence of that moment — when an instrument takes a measurement, it sets a precision ceiling that travels through every calculation that follows. Significant figures are how you stay honest about what your instruments actually knew. The more automatic this reasoning becomes, the more reliable your lab work will be.
