The case for this approach

Customized activities, built around your content.

Most ready-made interactive content is built for a general audience and a general topic — not your students, your terminology, or your course's specific outcomes. What makes this approach different is that every activity is generated from the specific content, outcomes, and context you provide. A branching scenario can reflect the exact clinical population your nursing students will encounter. A sorting activity can use the terminology your students will see on their assessments. The interactivity isn't a wrapper around generic content — it's designed to build mastery of the exact skills you'll be assessing.

There's a structural advantage here as well. Traditional interactive content often depends on authoring tools, LTI integrations, or external platforms that require institutional setup and ongoing maintenance. This workflow generates standard HTML that embeds directly in your LMS document editor, with nothing to install, configure, or maintain separately. That portability is a deliberate choice, and it's what makes the approach realistic to sustain and straightforward to share across a team.

The Methodology

Iteration is the process, not a step in it.

The goal isn't to generate HTML — it's to create purposeful, accessible learning experiences efficiently. That requires a specific way of working with AI.

01
Start with the learning need

Identify where content is flat, passive, or difficult for students to navigate. The interaction format should emerge from that need. Sometimes it helps to describe the content to an AI tool and ask what formats might work before specifying one. You may find options worth considering that you hadn't thought of.

02
Prompt with context and constraints

Effective prompts include the learning objective, the audience, and the technical parameters: single-file HTML, no external libraries, compatible with your LMS editor. The more specific the context, the more usable the output tends to be.

03
Test, refine, and document

AI-generated HTML almost always benefits from at least one round of targeted refinement. Test in your actual LMS environment. Document what works — both the prompts and the finished activities — so the workflow is repeatable by others on your team and transferable to faculty.

Implementation

What adoption could look like at your institution.

This workflow scales across resource levels. The models below reflect realistic starting points depending on your team's capacity and institutional context.

Individual Practitioner
  • An ID or faculty member adopts the workflow independently, using this site as a reference
  • Builds a personal library of activities for frequently taught courses
  • Uses AI iteratively to refine and troubleshoot without needing additional technical support
  • Lowest barrier to entry — requires only an AI tool and LMS access
ID Team or CTL
  • Shared prompt library and template standards maintained by the team
  • Accessibility review built into the design consultation workflow
  • Done-for-you activity support for faculty, with documentation for replication
  • Workshops or course design labs to build faculty capacity over time
Institutional Repository
  • Activities housed in a Blackboard Organization or shared LMS space for faculty access
  • Master course shells or templates with pre-built interactive content
  • Standardized naming conventions for discoverability and maintenance
  • Update workflows and version control for long-term sustainability
QA & Accessibility at Scale
  • Prompt templates with accessibility requirements baked in from the start
  • Alternate format or pathway provided when interactive content can't be made fully accessible
  • Team-level review process before activities go live in courses
  • Ongoing audit process as LMS environments and standards evolve
A note on tool choice: This workflow uses AI tools to generate standard HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — the same languages that power the web. Activities require no external libraries, no plugins, and no server-side dependencies. They embed directly into LMS document editors that support HTML input, which makes the workflow portable across institutions and maintainable without specialized infrastructure.
